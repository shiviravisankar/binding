Mismatch

How Inclusion Shapes Design

Overview

Sometimes designed objects reject their users, products and infrastructures designed by designers leave out entire populations who cannot use them. A computer mouse that doesn't work for left-handed people, or a touchscreen payment system that only works for people who read English phrases, have 20/20 vision, and use a credit card. People in wheelchairs for whom access to certain buildings is impossible. Similarly, racialized people are confronted with facial recognition systems that are set up to distinguish only white-skinned people.The small size of some women forces them to wear shoes in the children's department. Each of these examples is a mismatch, a design anomaly that deprives a group of people of the use of a product. From these mismatches comes a strong feeling of exclusion. How can we proceed to design and innovate in an inclusive way?

The book provides 3 main takeaways,

  • Learn to recognize exclusion and when it occurs.
  • Take the right inferences from diversity.
  • Solve for one, extend it across many.
